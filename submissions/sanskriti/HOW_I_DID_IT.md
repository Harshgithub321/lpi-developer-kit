## What I Learned

1. **SMILE is not about data volume — it's about decision framing**
   - Both case studies and sports show this: you don't need sensors everywhere. You need sensors and ontology focused on decisions that matter.

2. **Ontology is the most transferable asset**
   - The automotive case study's failure mode ontology outlasted the predictive model. In sports, codifying coach knowledge (injury patterns, overtraining signatures, tactical load) is more durable than any dashboard.

3. **Context beats raw metrics**
   - A high heart rate is not inherently good or bad. High load is not inherently harmful. The twin's value is knowing context: recent travel, match schedule, player history, sleep, life stress. This applies identically in healthcare, manufacturing, and sports.

4. **Phases are sequential but not rigid**
   - The healthcare case study (cs-004) deliberately stopped at Phase 4 (clinical governance for AI-generated recommendations was not yet established). This is realistic and smart. In sports, a club might stop at Phase 4 (dashboards for decision support) and skip Phase 5-6 (autonomous recommendations). The framework flexes.

5. **Interoperability is harder than data collection**
   - The urban mobility case study (cs-007) spent more time on legal and organizational interoperability than technical systems. In sports, getting coaches, medical staff, and data analysts to share a common ontology and trust the outputs is harder than plugging in sensors.
